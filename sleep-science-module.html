The Problem
Why Modern Life Wrecks Your Sleep
Click a disruptor to reveal the science.
🔋
Blue Light
Your screens are lying to your brain.
Blue Light Exposure
Blue light from phones, laptops, and TVs directly suppresses melatonin production. Your brain interprets blue light as daylight, delaying the sleep signal by up to 3 hours. Even low-level exposure for 2 hours before bed measurably reduces sleep quality.
Melatonin suppressed up to 3 hours
🤓
Stress and Cortisol
Your nervous system doesn't clock off.
Stress and Cortisol
Chronic stress keeps cortisol elevated into the evening. Since cortisol and melatonin are opposites in the body, high evening cortisol actively prevents sleep onset. Rumination keeps the prefrontal cortex active, which also delays sleep.
Chronic stress reduces deep sleep by 30%
☕
Caffeine
That 3pm coffee is still active at midnight.
Caffeine Half-Life
Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours. A coffee at 3pm means half the caffeine is still in your system at 9pm. It works by blocking adenosine receptors, preventing the buildup of sleep pressure that naturally drives you to sleep.
Half-life: 5 to 6 hours
📅
Irregular Schedules
Social jetlag is a real phenomenon.
Social Jetlag
Sleeping in on weekends and staying up late disrupts your circadian rhythm in the same way that flying across time zones does. Researchers call this social jetlag. Even a 1-2 hour shift in sleep timing on weekends is associated with higher rates of obesity, depression, and cardiovascular disease.
Affects 70% of the working population
True or False
Common Sleep Myths
Select True or False for each statement.
"You can catch up on lost sleep by sleeping in at the weekend."
False. While extra sleep can partially reduce sleepiness, research shows cognitive impairments from sleep debt cannot be fully reversed by weekend recovery sleep. Consistent sleep timing matters more than total hours.
"Adults need 7 to 9 hours of sleep per night for optimal health."
True. The National Sleep Foundation and American Academy of Sleep Medicine both recommend 7-9 hours for adults. Consistently sleeping less than 7 hours is associated with significantly increased risk of chronic disease.
"Alcohol helps you sleep better because it makes you drowsy."
False. While alcohol induces drowsiness, it suppresses REM sleep and causes sleep fragmentation in the second half of the night. You may fall asleep faster, but overall sleep quality is significantly reduced.

Memory, Focus, Decision Making
Sleep deprivation impairs the prefrontal cortex, reducing working memory, focus, and decision-making quality. After 17 hours awake, cognitive performance equals being legally drunk (0.05% BAC).

Heart and Cardiovascular System
Blood Pressure, Heart Rate, Risk
Chronic short sleep is associated with a 48% increased risk of heart disease. Blood pressure stays elevated longer, arterial inflammation increases, and the heart has less time to recover overnight.
48% increased risk of heart disease
Immune System
Infection Resistance, Recovery
People sleeping less than 7 hours are nearly 3 times more likely to catch a cold when exposed to the virus. Sleep is when the immune system produces cytokines, proteins that fight infection and inflammation.
3x more likely to catch a cold
Metabolism and Weight
Hunger Hormones, Blood Sugar
Sleep loss increases ghrelin (hunger hormone) and decreases leptin (satiety hormone), driving overeating. Even one week of short sleep can produce insulin resistance comparable to pre-diabetic states.
Hunger increases by up to 24% after poor sleep
Emotional Regulation
Mood, Anxiety, Reactivity
The amygdala becomes 60% more reactive after one night of poor sleep. The connection between the amygdala and prefrontal cortex weakens, reducing your ability to regulate emotional responses and increasing anxiety.
Amygdala reactivity increases 60%

Evidence-Based Solutions
5 Strategies Backed by the Research
⏰
Consistent Sleep Timing
Going to bed and waking at the same time every day, including weekends, is the single most impactful sleep hygiene intervention. Consistency anchors your circadian rhythm, reduces sleep onset time, and increases slow-wave sleep.
Try this: Set a wake time and keep it for 2 weeks, even if you had a bad night. Sleep onset will improve naturally.
The Science
Chronobiology research shows that the timing of sleep matters as much as duration. Irregular sleep timing desynchronises peripheral clocks in organs from the central clock in the brain, producing metabolic and cognitive consequences similar to chronic shift work.
🔋
Strategic Light Management
Get bright light exposure within 30 minutes of waking (outside is best). Dim indoor lights after 8pm and use blue-light filtering after sunset. This single intervention can advance melatonin onset by up to 90 minutes.
Try this: Eat breakfast near a window or step outside for 10 minutes in the morning. Use warm-toned lamps in the evening.
The Science
The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), your master clock, is reset daily by light exposure through the retinohypothalamic tract. Morning light suppresses residual melatonin and anchors cortisol timing. Evening light delays both signals, pushing sleep later.
❄
Bedroom Temperature
Your core body temperature needs to drop by 1 to 3 degrees Celsius to initiate and maintain sleep. A cooler bedroom accelerates this process. The optimal sleep temperature for most people is 16 to 19 degrees Celsius.
Try this: Keep your bedroom between 16-19C. A warm bath or shower 1-2 hours before bed paradoxically accelerates sleep onset by drawing heat to the skin.
The Science
Sleep onset is tightly coupled to core temperature decline. Thermosensitive neurons in the hypothalamus detect this drop and promote slow-wave sleep. A warm bath works by vasodilation, pulling heat to the body surface and accelerating core cooling after you get out.
🌞
A Consistent Wind-Down Routine
Your nervous system needs a transition from activation to deactivation. A 30 to 60 minute wind-down routine signals safety to the amygdala, reduces cortisol, and activates the parasympathetic nervous system, making sleep onset faster and deeper.
Try this: Choose 2 to 3 calming activities (reading, light stretching, journalling) and do them in the same order every night. Predictability is the mechanism.
The Science
Sleep onset requires the transition from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) to parasympathetic (rest and digest) nervous system dominance. Conditioned relaxation cues, through consistent evening routines, create this shift more reliably than willpower or trying to force sleep directly.
📝
Cognitive Offloading
Rumination and racing thoughts are among the most common causes of sleep onset insomnia. Writing down tomorrow's tasks and concerns before bed reduces cognitive load and signal to the brain that the problem has been acknowledged and set aside.
Try this: Spend 5 minutes writing a to-do list for tomorrow before bed. Research shows this reduces sleep onset time by an average of 9 minutes.
The Science
A 2018 study at Baylor University found that writing a specific to-do list before bed reduced sleep onset time by an average of 9 minutes compared to journalling about completed tasks. The specificity of the list was key, more specific planning produced faster sleep onset.
