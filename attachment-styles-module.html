Attachment Theory in the Workplace
Select an attachment style from the menu to explore how it shows up at work.
Secure Attachment

People with a secure attachment style feel comfortable with both closeness and independence at work. They trust colleagues, communicate needs directly, and handle conflict without excessive anxiety or avoidance.

In the workplace, securely attached professionals tend to give and receive feedback well, maintain healthy boundaries, collaborate effectively, and recover from setbacks without prolonged distress.

Secure attachment is not about being perfect. It is about feeling fundamentally safe in relationships, even when things get difficult.

Anxious Attachment

People with an anxious attachment style crave closeness and reassurance at work but worry that others do not value them as much as they value others. This can show up as a strong need for approval and difficulty tolerating uncertainty.

In the workplace, anxiously attached professionals may read too much into a manager's tone, feel disproportionately affected by critical feedback, or seek frequent check-ins for reassurance.

With awareness, anxious attachment can be managed. The key is learning to build internal security rather than seeking it entirely from external validation.

Avoidant Attachment

People with an avoidant attachment style value independence highly and feel uncomfortable with too much closeness or emotional expression at work. They tend to pull back when relationships feel too demanding and prefer to handle problems alone.

In the workplace, avoidantly attached professionals may seem cold or distant to colleagues, resist asking for help even when they need it, or shut down during emotionally charged conversations.

Avoidant attachment is not the same as introversion. It is a protective strategy, and with awareness it can be softened to allow for more genuine connection.

Disorganised Attachment

Disorganised attachment involves a conflict between wanting closeness and fearing it at the same time. People with this style often had early experiences where caregivers were both a source of comfort and fear, leaving them without a consistent strategy for managing relationships.

In the workplace, this can show up as unpredictable behaviour, difficulty trusting others, heightened reactivity to perceived rejection, and feeling overwhelmed in emotionally complex situations.

Disorganised attachment is not a life sentence. With support, people can develop much more secure relational patterns over time.

Moving Towards Secure

Attachment styles are not fixed. Research in adult attachment theory shows that people can develop more secure patterns over time through meaningful relationships, therapy, coaching, and deliberate self-awareness.

This process is called earned security. It does not require a perfect past. It requires consistent experiences of being heard, supported, and safe, which can happen at any age and at any stage of your career.

Awareness is always the first step. The fact that you are here, exploring this, already puts you ahead.
